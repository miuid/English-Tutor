# Educational Policy and Pedagogical Report: Year 8 and 9 English Standards, Assessments, and AI-Driven Tutoring Frameworks in Queensland

## Structural Framework of the Queensland English Curriculum

The English curriculum for Year 8 and Year 9 in Queensland is governed by the Queensland Curriculum and Assessment Authority (QCAA) in alignment with Version 9.0 of the Australian Curriculum. The secondary English curriculum is structured around three interrelated strands: Language, Literature, and Literacy. Together, these three strands develop a student's capacity across six core language modes: listening, reading, viewing, speaking, writing, and creating.

The curriculum assumes a developmental progression. Content is systematically revisited, consolidated, and expanded using texts of increasing complexity and more abstract thematic concepts as students progress from Year 8 to Year 9. This transition serves as a critical developmental pathway, preparing students for senior secondary subjects including Senior English, Literature, and Essential English in Years 11 and 12.

The QCAA integrates the national Literacy General Capability learning progression to provide a detailed, fine-grained description of literacy development alongside the core English curriculum. This progression is structured into three primary elements: Speaking and listening, Reading and viewing, and Writing. These elements are further divided into sub-elements that define the mechanical and conceptual benchmarks required of secondary students.

| ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Speaking and Listening**[cite: 8] | Speaking and listening interactions                          | Managing spoken presentations, participating in panel discussions, rehearsing, and delivering analytical arguments. |

| **Writing**[cite: 8]                | Creating texts, grammar, punctuation, spelling, handwriting and keyboarding | Developing fluent, cohesive, and grammatically accurate compositions, utilizing keyboarding or legible handwriting to draft and edit. |



In Year 8, students are expected to recognize how language shapes relationships, analyze literary devices such as similes and metaphors, and investigate how visual texts use intertextual references to create layers of meaning. By the end of Year 8, students must demonstrate the ability to select and vary text structures to develop, link, and organize ideas with supporting evidence.

As students transition into Year 9, the academic expectations shift from explanation to critical analysis. Year 9 students must analyze representations of people, places, events, and concepts, evaluating how texts respond to diverse historical and social contexts. They are expected to experiment with text structures and language features, manipulating elements of voice, tone, and multimodal features to position audiences.

## Pedagogical Methodologies in Queensland Schools

The Queensland Department of Education requires a systematic, whole-school approach to pedagogy to align curriculum delivery, classroom instruction, assessment, and reporting. Queensland schools utilize a range of teaching approaches to cater to diverse student cohorts and ensure consistent achievement.

### Systematic Pedagogical Approaches

The state's pedagogical framework outlines several distinct approaches that are blended to match students' developmental needs:

- **Explicit Instruction:** A highly structured and systematic approach to teaching academic skills. It breaks complex tasks down into clear steps, providing explanations, modeling, and guided practice.
- **Direct Teaching/Instruction:** A scripted, lesson-by-lesson approach that follows a pre-determined skill acquisition sequence to guarantee basic literacy development.
- **Inquiry and Project Approaches:** Inquiry-based learning starts with a central question or problem, encouraging students to research, evaluate perspectives, and arrive at independent conclusions. Project-based learning involves in-depth, long-term exploration of complex topics.
- **Blended Approach:** Secondary schools routinely utilize a blended approach, combining explicit instruction for mechanical skills (such as grammar and spelling) with inquiry-based models for thematic and literary analysis.

### The Gradual Release of Responsibility (GRR) Model

The fundamental framework for delivering explicit instruction in secondary English is the Gradual Release of Responsibility (GRR) model, commonly referred to as "I Do, We Do, You Do". This model transitions the cognitive load of learning from teacher-led demonstration to student-led execution.

- **Modelled Practice ("I Do"):** The teacher demonstrates the skill explicitly. During this phase, the teacher uses "think-alouds" to model cognitive behaviors, such as analyzing the symbolic qualities of a poem or identifying correct punctuation.
- **Guided Collaboration ("We Do"):** The teacher and students work together on the task, allowing students to practice with direct guidance. This phase features an extended handover, where structural scaffolds are slowly reduced based on student readiness.
- **Independent Practice ("You Do"):** Students apply their newly acquired knowledge independently. The teacher monitors student work and provides targeted feedback, ensuring that students take ownership of their learning.

### Reading Comprehension and Analytical Writing Strategies

Secondary English programs utilize structured frameworks to develop reading comprehension and clear academic writing:

- **Reciprocal Reading:** An interactive strategy designed to improve reading comprehension through structured student dialogue. Students rotate through four key roles: *Summarising* key plot points or content; *Formulating questions* about thematic or literal meanings; *Clarifying* complex vocabulary or idioms; and *Predicting* subsequent text developments.
- **Question-Answer Relationships (QAR):** This framework teaches students to identify the source of information needed to answer comprehension questions, categorizing them into four distinct levels of cognitive processing:
  - *Right There:* Literal questions where the answer is explicitly stated in a single sentence of the text.
  - *Think and Search:* Simple inferential questions where the answer is scattered across multiple sentences or paragraphs, requiring synthesis.
  - *Author and Me:* Higher-order inferential questions requiring students to combine clues from the text with their own prior knowledge.
  - *On My Own:* Evaluative questions where the text serves as a starting point, and students respond using their own personal and conceptual frameworks.
- **PEEL / TEEL Paragraph Structure:** A writing framework that ensures students construct logical, cohesive, and evidence-supported paragraphs. The acronym represents **P**oint (or Topic Sentence), **E**xplanation (or Elaboration), **E**vidence (or direct Example/Quote), and **L**ink (reconnecting the paragraph back to the overall thesis statement).

### Support for Diverse Learners (EAL/D Framework)

To support students from culturally and linguistically diverse backgrounds, Queensland schools provide specialized English as an Additional Language or Dialect (EAL/D) support. EAL/D learners are identified during enrollment or through ongoing classroom observations.

Teachers utilize the *Bandscales State Schools (Queensland)* framework to assess EAL/D students' language proficiency across listening, speaking, reading, and writing. While EAL/D students are taught the standard, age-appropriate Australian Curriculum, teachers adjust assessment demands based on their language level, allowing them to demonstrate content understanding through alternative formats like illustrations, diagrams, or verbal presentations.

## Evaluative Standards and Marking Rubrics

To determine student grades, Queensland teachers evaluate student work on a five-point scale (A to E) using standard elaborations based on the Australian Curriculum achievement standards. The C standard represents a sound level of knowledge and understanding, while A and B grades require purposeful, highly effective, and sophisticated performance.

### Marking Criteria (Year 8 vs. Year 9)

Assessments are marked across three core criteria:

#### 1. Listening, Speaking, and Creating

- **Grade A (Year 8):** *Purposefully* discusses, expresses, and elaborates on ideas with supporting evidence; uses *purposeful* variation of text structures, language features, and *considered* features of voice to deliver multimodal presentations.
- **Grade A (Year 9):** *Purposefully* discusses and expands on ideas, shaping meaning and providing substantiation; demonstrates *purposeful* selection and *considered* experimentation with text structures, language features, and features of voice.
- **Grade C (Year 8):** Standardly discusses and elaborates on ideas with supporting evidence; uses standard text structures and language features.
- **Grade C (Year 9):** Standardly discusses and expands on ideas, showing standard selection of text structures and language features.
- **Grade E (Year 8):** Relies on basic *statements* of ideas with *fragmented* text structures and *sporadic* use of language features or voice.
- **Grade E (Year 9):** Relies on *sporadic* discussion and *fragmented* structures to organize basic ideas.

#### 2. Reading and Viewing

- **Grade A (Year 8):** Provides a *considered explanation* of how ideas are represented, how texts reflect or challenge contexts, how structures shape meaning, and the effects of language and visual features.
- **Grade A (Year 9):** Demonstrates *considered analysis* of representations, how texts respond to contexts, aesthetic qualities, and the effects of text structures and language features.
- **Grade C (Year 8):** Explains standard text representations, structural elements, and the effects of basic language features.
- **Grade C (Year 9):** Standardly analyzes representations of people, places, events, and concepts.
- **Grade E (Year 8):** Limited to simple *statements* about ideas, contexts, and obvious visual elements.
- **Grade E (Year 9):** Restricted to basic *statements* about obvious representations and literal text details.

#### 3. Writing and Creating

- **Grade A (Year 8):** Demonstrates *purposeful creation* of texts to express and advance ideas with supporting evidence; uses *purposeful* variation of text structures and language features.
- **Grade A (Year 9):** Shows *purposeful creation* of written and multimodal texts, expressing and expanding ideas with strong substantiation; demonstrates *purposeful* selection and experimentation with text structures and language features.
- **Grade C (Year 8):** Standardly creates written texts to express and advance ideas with supporting evidence, using standard structures and language features.
- **Grade C (Year 9):** Standardly creates written and multimodal texts to express and expand ideas, using standard structures and language features.
- **Grade E (Year 8):** Creates basic texts using simple *statements* of ideas, relying on *fragmented* structures and *sporadic* language features.
- **Grade E (Year 9):** Produces *sporadic* written or multimodal texts with *fragmented* structures.

### The NAPLAN Writing Evaluation Scale

In Year 9, students participate in the national NAPLAN Writing assessment. Submissions are evaluated against ten criteria, yielding a maximum score of 47 marks.

The total score $S_{\text{total}}$ is calculated as:

$$S_{\text{total}} = A + \text{TS} + I + \text{CD} + V + C + P + \text{SS} + \text{Pu} + \text{Sp}$$

Where each score maps to specific developmental rubrics:

$$\begin{aligned} A &\in [0, 6] \quad \text{(Audience: The writer's capacity to orient, engage, and affect the reader)} \\ \text{TS} &\in [0, 4] \quad \text{(Text Structure: Organization of narrative or persuasive structures)} \\ I &\in [0, 5] \quad \text{(Ideas: The generation, selection, and structural crafting of ideas)} \\ \text{CD} &\in [0, 4] \quad \text{(Character \& Setting / Persuasive Devices: Contextual development)} \\ V &\in [0, 5] \quad \text{(Vocabulary: Focuses on the range and precision of language choices)} \\ C &\in [0, 4] \quad \text{(Cohesion: The control of multiple threads and semantic links)} \\ P &\in [0, 2] \quad \text{(Paragraphing: The logical grouping and segmenting of ideas)} \\ \text{SS} &\in [0, 6] \quad \text{(Sentence Structure: Production of grammatically sound sentences)} \\ \text{Pu} &\in [0, 5] \quad \text{(Punctuation: Range and correct application of punctuation)} \\ \text{Sp} &\in [0, 6] \quad \text{(Spelling: Accurate selection and arrangement of letters)} \end{aligned}$$

## AI Tutoring System Design and Skill Capabilities

To replicate a high-quality classroom environment, an AI home tutoring agent must utilize standard Queensland pedagogical frameworks. The agent should operate as an interactive mentor, guiding students through scaffolded challenges rather than simply providing answers.

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    AI TUTORING COGNITION                   │
│                                                            │
│  [1] Diagnostics                                           │
│      - Formative reading and writing checkpoints.          │
│                                                            │
│  [2] Scaffolding                                           │
│      - Grad Release model: Model, prompt, fade.            │
│                                                            │
│  [3] Text Integration                                      │
│      - Evaluates evidence linking, PEEL, citation.         │
│                                                            │
│  [4] Constructive Feedback                                 │
│      - Detailed grading against QCAA matrix benchmarks.    │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The AI agent must be configured with four core pedagogical skills:

### 1. Diagnostic Literacy Profiling

The agent must assess the student's current performance across writing structures, vocabulary, grammar, and reading comprehension. By diagnosing areas where the student is working at a C or D standard—such as fragmented paragraph transitions, basic clause structures, or literal interpretation—the agent can customize its daily lessons to target these specific needs.

### 2. Scaffolded Instruction (Gradual Release Delivery)

The agent must actively use the Gradual Release of Responsibility (GRR) framework. It should structure exercises to first demonstrate a skill (modeling), then guide the student through structured practice (scaffolding), and finally transition to independent prompts (independent execution) as the student shows progress.

### 3. Systematic Mentoring of PEEL & QAR Structures

The agent must guide the student through structured analytical writing. It should help students organize body paragraphs using the PEEL template, ensuring they formulate clear arguments and link direct textual evidence back to a central thesis statement.

For reading comprehension, the agent must use the Question-Answer Relationships (QAR) framework, helping students distinguish between literal ("Right there") and inferential ("Author and me") questions to improve their reading comprehension.

### 4. Objective, Criteria-Aligned Feedback

The agent must analyze student writing using the precise language of the QCAA Standard Elaborations and the NAPLAN Writing criteria. Feedback should point out exact grammatical, structural, or conceptual errors, providing constructive steps to help the student elevate their work to an A-grade standard.
