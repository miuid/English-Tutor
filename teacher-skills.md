# Create Skills

You can turn each evidence-based teacher skill into concrete “agent skills” that support your child’s English learning from Year 8 to Year 12. The skills below are all drawn from Australian Professional Standards for Teachers (APST), High Impact Teaching Strategies (HITS) and the Victorian Teaching and Learning Model (VTLM), not guesses.[[www2.education.vic.gov](https://www2.education.vic.gov.au/pal/victorian-teaching-learning-model/guidance)]

------

## Subject knowledge & pedagogy

Research on APST Standard 2 shows that effective teachers must understand the concepts, substance and structure of their teaching area, and be able to choose appropriate teaching strategies for that content. For English, guidance on integrating literacy and subject knowledge emphasises building understanding of both content (e.g. themes, text structures) and language (e.g. vocabulary, grammar) through staged and scaffolded activities. An English agent therefore needs a mapped model of the 7–12 English curriculum (text types, literary devices, grammar, vocabulary) plus the ability to explain these clearly and sequence explanations from simpler to more complex concepts.[[education.vic.gov](https://www.education.vic.gov.au/Documents/school/teachers/teachingresources/practice/Professional_Practice_Note_9_HITS.docx)]

------

## Lesson planning & structuring

Evidence-based frameworks such as HITS and VTLM state that effective teachers “plan and deliver structured lessons which incorporate a series of clear steps and transitions between them, and scaffold learning to build students’ knowledge and skills.” VTLM guidance describes using advance organisers, activating prior knowledge and managing cognitive load so new content is introduced in logically ordered chunks. An agent skill here is the ability to design and adjust a sequence like “quick retrieval / explicit explanation / guided practice / independent practice / brief review” for reading or writing tasks, and to reorder or repeat steps based on how your child is performing.[[arc.educationapps.vic.gov](https://arc.educationapps.vic.gov.au/49149.rsf)]

------

## Goal setting (learning intentions & success criteria)

HITS identifies “setting goals” as a high-impact strategy where teachers set and communicate clear lesson goals and success criteria so students commit to the learning and understand what success looks like. VTLM explicit teaching guidance recommends stating learning objectives in student-friendly language and breaking them into “I can …” success criteria linked to curriculum descriptors. An English agent should be able to generate specific goals for each session (e.g. “We are learning to identify and explain imagery in a poem” with success criteria such as “I can find two examples of imagery and explain their effect”) and prompt your child to self-check against these at the end of the session.[[evidenceforlearning.org](https://evidenceforlearning.org.au/education-evidence/aligned-evidence/hits)]

------

## Explicit teaching & modelling

VTLM 2.0 and HITS position explicit teaching as a core evidence-based practice in which teachers fully explain and demonstrate what students need to learn, breaking content into manageable parts and using worked examples and frequent checks for understanding. Guidance emphasises the “I do, we do, you do” progression and scaffolding, using sentence stems, graphic organisers, worked examples and other supports, then gradually withdrawing them as students gain proficiency. An English agent needs to implement that same pattern: first modelling a reading or writing strategy on a text, then guiding your child through similar tasks with prompts and scaffolds, and finally asking them to complete comparable tasks independently while the agent checks understanding and gives corrective feedback.[[education.vic.gov](https://www.education.vic.gov.au/Documents/school/teachers/support/high-impact-teaching-strategies.pdf)]

------

## Assessment & feedback

APST Standard 5 and NESA professional development materials stress that effective teachers use a mix of informal and formal diagnostic, formative and summative assessments to monitor student learning and then provide timely, effective and appropriate feedback aligned with learning goals. HITS describes feedback as a high-impact strategy where two‑way feedback both advances students’ learning and verifies the impact of teaching, with guidance that feedback should be specific, timely and linked to success criteria. For an English agent, this means embedding regular low‑stakes assessments (short comprehension checks, vocabulary tasks, paragraph writing) and then giving immediate, criterion‑referenced feedback on content, structure and language while also asking your child to explain their thinking or reflect on what they found hard.[[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jx4_aAR1mtY)][[education.nsw.gov](https://education.nsw.gov.au/teaching-and-learning/education-for-a-changing-world/thinking-skills/metacognition---a-key-to-unlocking-learning)]

------

## Differentiated teaching

The HITS documentation and Victorian literacy resources show that differentiated teaching, supported by Response to Intervention or multi‑tier systems, produces large positive effects by tailoring instruction to students’ readiness and needs. Effective differentiation involves using formative assessment data to group students flexibly, adjust task difficulty and sequence, and apply targeted interventions, while assessing students against their prior achievement rather than against other students. An English agent should therefore diagnose your child’s current reading and writing level, vary text complexity and task demands (e.g. sentence-level work vs essay planning), and dynamically add or remove scaffolds and extension tasks so they experience both success and challenge as they progress.[[thecenterforcharters](https://www.thecenterforcharters.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/HighImpactTeachingStrategies.pdf)]

------

## Evidence‑informed and metacognitive practice

VTLM guidance on explicit teaching and “monitor progress” describes responsive teaching—adapting instruction based on evidence from observation and formative assessment, including adjusting scaffolds, revisiting explanations and extending capable students. NSW’s “Metacognition: a key to unlocking learning” report summarises the evidence that teaching students to plan, monitor and evaluate their learning strategies improves achievement and self‑regulation across subjects, including English. An English agent thus needs to track your child’s performance over time (error patterns, strengths), use that data to select future texts and tasks, and coach them in metacognitive moves like setting reading goals, monitoring comprehension, and reflecting on which writing strategies helped or hindered them.[[education.nsw.gov](https://education.nsw.gov.au/teaching-and-learning/education-for-a-changing-world/thinking-skills/metacognition---a-key-to-unlocking-learning)]

------

## Agent design overview

Together, these research-backed skills suggest an English agent that can: map curriculum-aligned content, structure sessions, set clear goals, teach explicitly using I‑do/we‑do/you‑do, assess and give feedback, differentiate tasks, and adapt using evidence and metacognitive coaching. # The Professional Competency Framework for Secondary English Educators in Australian Independent Schools: An Evidence-Based Synthesis of Essential Skills

Successful curriculum delivery within the non-government secondary school sector in Australia requires a highly specialized blend of clinical pedagogical skill, strict compliance with national standards, and active alignment with institutional philosophies. Rather than acting as isolated classroom practitioners, English teachers in modern independent schools function as critical drivers of student academic outcomes, literacy leaders, and holistic mentors. This professional reality is shaped by evolving external accountabilities, declining national writing metrics, and high community expectations.

To build a sustainable and impactful career in this environment, an educator must possess a multidimensional framework of proven, evidence-backed competencies. Every capability required for success is grounded in official mandates from national and state educational authorities, such as the Australian Institute for Teaching and School Leadership (AITSL) and the Queensland Curriculum and Assessment Authority (QCAA), alongside extensive research from institutions like the Australian Education Research Organisation (AERO) and the Australian Council for Educational Research (ACER).

## Professional Knowledge Standards and Regulatory Compliance

In the Australian education landscape, professional excellence is codified through the Australian Professional Standards for Teachers (APST), which serve as the definitive framework for teacher quality and career progression. Managed by AITSL, these standards govern teacher accreditation across four career stages: Graduate, Proficient, Highly Accomplished, and Lead.

A successful English teacher in an independent school must demonstrate consistent, high-level competency across the three core domains of the APST: Professional Knowledge, Professional Practice, and Professional Engagement.

### The Australian Professional Standards for Teachers Framework

The professional teaching journey is legally tethered to the APST. Achieving and maintaining full teacher registration with state bodies, such as the Queensland College of Teachers (QCT) or the NSW Education Standards Authority (NESA), is contingent upon a teacher’s ability to document their practice against these standards. Under standard two, which demands deep content and pedagogical knowledge, an English teacher must demonstrate a nuanced understanding of textual structures and language modes.

Furthermore, standard five requires teachers to interpret complex student assessment data to evaluate learning and modify their instruction accordingly. National research suggests that teacher quality remains the single most critical in-school factor influencing student achievement, making a teacher's alignment with the APST a vital component of school-wide excellence.

### Suitability to Teach and Child Protection Mandates

Beyond academic qualifications, teachers in the independent sector are held to exceptionally high standards of personal, ethical, and professional conduct. Regulatory frameworks like the suitability-to-teach assessments managed by the QCT evaluate an educator's character, integrity, and compliance both inside and outside the classroom. The paramount consideration is always the safety, welfare, and best interests of children.

Consequently, teachers must maintain active working-with-children clearance, such as the Queensland Blue Card or exemption card, and strictly adhere to statutory child safe standards. Independent schools, such as the Anglican Church Grammar School (Churchie) and Padua College, integrate these compliance checks directly into their recruitment and selection criteria, evaluating an applicant’s commitment to ethical professional boundaries, student safety, and protective behaviors.

## Subject-Specific Pedagogical and Textual Competency

Within the secondary school environment, secondary English teachers face a unique professional paradox: while they are expected to navigate highly sophisticated senior courses, out-of-field teaching remains a prominent challenge in the wider sector. Recent research on job advertisements in non-government schools indicates that only about a quarter of positions explicitly demand a major or minor university qualification in English.

This creates a high demand for genuine subject specialists who possess a deep foundation in English literature, literary criticism, and text selection. Successful educators must navigate distinct roles, serving as literature experts for high-achieving cohorts, literacy supporters for struggling learners, and whole-school literacy leaders who assist colleagues across all learning areas.

### The Secondary English Curriculum Matrix

The senior secondary curriculum requires a highly technical understanding of different syllabus pathways. In states like Queensland, the QCAA structures senior English into five distinct subjects, each catering to different student trajectories and requiring specific teacher expertise.

Successful curriculum delivery is dependent on the teacher's ability to plan learning programs that address the official objectives of these pathways, ensuring that students can analyze, create, and defend texts across various modes and mediums.

## Cognitive Science and Evidence-Based Pedagogy

In response to research compiled by educational authorities, independent schools in Queensland and nationally are moving away from unstructured, inquiry-only teaching models. Instead, robust school-based pedagogical frameworks are increasingly aligned with cognitive science, neuroscience, and educational psychology.

The Independent Schools Queensland (ISQ) guidelines emphasize that a high-performing pedagogical model must establish a shared instructional language across the school, linking research-tested principles to concrete classroom techniques.

### Principles of Memory and Cognitive Load Management

A robust pedagogical approach is built upon a scientific understanding of the human mind, often drawing on cognitive models popular in independent school frameworks, such as those by Daniel Willingham or Tom Sherrington. Research compiled by AERO highlights that learning represents a permanent change in long-term memory. Because human working memory is severely limited and can only process a small amount of new information at one time, teachers must master cognitive load management.

This requires a clear distinction between a pedagogical *principle* (the cognitive reason why an action is taken) and a *technique* (the physical strategy used in the classroom). For instance, a teacher may use a technique like mini-whiteboards or a structured "turn-and-talk" activity to enact the principle of maximizing active participation and regular checks for understanding, ensuring that incorrect information is not encoded into long-term memory.

### Systematic Phonics and Reading Intervention

Cognitive science evidence has profound implications for how reading and basic literacy must be addressed in high schools. National data from the Australian Education Research Organisation and the Australian Council for Educational Research reveals that approximately one in five students enters secondary school at or below minimum reading standards. This literacy gap is cumulative, growing from one in five to one in four students between Years 7 and 9. Because secondary teachers are traditionally not trained in foundational reading skills, schools require teachers who can implement a Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS).

As established by the National Inquiry into the Teaching of Literacy, the foundation of reading proficiency relies on explicit, systematic phonics instruction. For older struggling readers, secondary English teachers must be equipped to implement targeted interventions that address decoding deficits, alongside systematic vocabulary instruction focusing on the etymology and morphology of academic language.

### Explicit Writing Instruction and AERO Model

The critical need for explicit teaching is further demonstrated by long-term academic trends. An analysis of a decade’s worth of NAPLAN data reveals a steady decline in persuasive writing proficiency among Australian secondary students, with the downward trend most pronounced in Years 7 and 9. In response, AERO developed the School Writing Instruction Framework (SWIF) and its corresponding Writing Instruction Model.

This evidence-based approach rejects naturalistic writing acquisition theories, instead advocating for explicit, systematic instruction across all subject areas. The model positions continuous formative assessment and honest, constructive feedback at its very center, requiring teachers to move through structured instructional phases.

| **Stage of AERO Writing Instruction Model**        | **Underlying Educational Mechanism**                         | **Practical Classroom Applications and Evidence**            |
| -------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **1. Build Knowledge & Language Skills**[cite: 11] | Constructing a rich cognitive schema so students have content to write about. | Leading whole-class readings of complex, knowledge-rich texts; directly addressing Tier 2 and Tier 3 academic vocabulary. |
| **2. Teach About the Genre**[cite: 11]             | Outlining how written forms function as social practices for specific audiences. | Deconstructing model texts; explicitly explaining structural conventions and how tone changes based on the target audience. |
| **3. Provide Explicit Instruction**[cite: 11]      | Applying the gradual release of responsibility to manage cognitive load. | **"I Do" (Modelling):** Live teacher writing paired with metathinking. **"We Do" (Joint Construction):** Co-constructing sentences using specific syntax prompts. |
| **4. Students Practise Writing**[cite: 11]         | Building automaticity in foundational sentence mechanics through spaced repetition. | **"You Do" (Independent Practice):** Allocating dedicated class time for extended, independent writing as structural scaffolds are removed. |
| **5. Assessment & Moderation**[cite: 11]           | Evaluating learning growth and aligning grading standards across classes. | Setting transparent writing rubrics; engaging in collaborative moderation of work portfolios with department peers. |



## Assessment Literacy and Quality Assurance Frameworks

In the Australian independent school sector, high academic performance is heavily dependent on a teacher’s assessment literacy. Under the QCAA senior certificate system, internal school-developed assessments are subject to rigorous state-wide quality assurance procedures.

An English teacher must possess the technical skills to design, administer, and grade assessments that satisfy these external benchmarks, ensuring both validity and accessibility.

### QCE Endorsement and Confirmation Standards

To maintain equity and comparability across Queensland, schools must navigate the official quality assurance cycle. This cycle consists of two distinct phases:

- **The Endorsement Process:** Before administering any summative internal assessment in General subjects, schools must submit three assessment instruments to the QCAA for approval. Teachers must design these instruments using rigid QCAA templates and quality assurance check tools. State assessors evaluate each submission to ensure it conforms to syllabus standards and provides all students with an equitable, accessible opportunity to demonstrate learning.
- **The Confirmation Process:** Once assessments are completed and marked by school staff, a quality assurance audit is conducted. Teachers submit provisional criteria-based marks to the QCAA via the Student Management portal. The QCAA then selects representative samples of student work, which must be uploaded alongside annotated, marked Instrument-Specific Marking Guides (ISMGs). State-appointed assessors review these samples to verify that the school’s marking aligns perfectly with national standards.

Furthermore, different rules apply depending on the year level. For Units 1 and 2, schools maintain autonomy to design internal grading systems, typically reporting student outcomes to the QCAA as Satisfactory (S), Unsatisfactory (U), or Not Rated (NR). For Units 3 and 4, teachers must award numerical marks based on the strict descriptors of the ISMGs, which are subsequently confirmed and combined with the external examination score to determine a final subject result out of 100.

Whether a school is a non-denominational grammar school emphasizing secular, liberal scholarship, or a religious college requiring adherence to specific statements of belief, the educator must actively support and reflect these values.

### Supporting Values-Based Education and Pastoral Care

Independent schools emphasize student wellbeing, requiring teachers to possess skills in trauma-informed practice, restorative justice, and inclusive education. Teachers must maintain safe and supportive environments, catering to diverse populations, including EAL/D learners, Indigenous students, and students identifying as LGBTQI+.

This pastoral care is integrated into daily operations, requiring teachers to maintain open, respectful, and confidential communication channels with parents and guardians. Additionally, teachers are expected to contribute to the co-curricular program by coaching sports, coordinating debating, or supporting outdoor education, viewing these activities as key opportunities for holistic student character development.

## Professional Growth, Collaboration, and Collective Efficacy

Successful educators in independent schools do not work in isolation; instead, they operate within high-functioning professional learning communities (PLCs). The impact of a teacher’s work is significantly enhanced when they engage in collaborative professionalism and align their development with school-wide strategic improvement goals.

### Professional Learning Communities and Appraisal Systems

Research indicates that collective teacher efficacy—the shared belief among a school staff that they can positively influence student learning outcomes, even for disengaged or disadvantaged cohorts—has a powerful impact on student achievement. High-performing independent schools foster this efficacy by establishing structured PLCs. Within these communities, teachers engage in the High-Quality Professional Learning (HQPL) cycle, identifying student needs, applying evidence-based strategies, and collaboratively analyzing classroom observation data.

Furthermore, elite schools use comprehensive performance planning and appraisal frameworks, such as the "MyCPD" system at Brisbane Girls Grammar School. These frameworks connect individual position descriptions to performance reviews, classroom observations, and reflective appraisal cycles conducted by school leaders.

## Conclusion

Success as an English Curriculum teacher in an Australian private high school is defined by a highly integrated professional framework. The evidence from national policy bodies and educational research indicates that teachers must possess a sophisticated command of national standards, state-level syllabus expectations, and cognitive science-based pedagogies.

By combining technical assessment literacy with a commitment to the school’s unique values, pastoral care systems, and collaborative professional learning communities, secondary English educators can meet high community standards and support student growth.
